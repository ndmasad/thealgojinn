Candlestick charts were invented by Japanese rice traders in the 18th century. Nearly 300 years later, they remain the most widely used chart type among professional traders worldwide — including on NSE and BSE. Here's why they work, and how to read them correctly.

## What is a candlestick?

Every candle on a chart represents price movement over a specific time period — whether that's 1 minute, 1 hour, or 1 day. Each candle has four components:

- **Open** — the price when the period started
- **Close** — the price when the period ended
- **High** — the highest price reached
- **Low** — the lowest price reached

The thick body of the candle shows the open-to-close range. The thin lines above and below (called wicks or shadows) show the high and low.

> A **green candle** means the close was higher than the open — buyers won. A **red candle** means the close was lower than the open — sellers won.

## Why candlestick patterns work

Candlestick patterns work because they reflect human psychology — fear, greed, indecision, and momentum. When the same patterns repeat across thousands of traders over decades, they become self-fulfilling to a degree.

That said — no pattern works 100% of the time. Always use them with other tools like volume, support/resistance, and trend direction.

## The 12 patterns every Indian trader must know

### 1. Doji

A Doji forms when the open and close are almost equal, creating a tiny body with long wicks. It signals **indecision** — neither buyers nor sellers are in control.

**What to do:** Wait for the next candle to confirm direction before acting.

### 2. Hammer

A small body at the top with a long lower wick (at least 2x the body). Forms after a downtrend and signals a potential **bullish reversal**.

**What to do:** Buy if the next candle is green and closes above the hammer's high.

### 3. Shooting Star

The opposite of a hammer — small body at the bottom, long upper wick. Forms after an uptrend and signals a potential **bearish reversal**.

**What to do:** Sell or stay out if the next candle confirms downward movement.

### 4. Bullish Engulfing

A large green candle that completely engulfs the previous red candle. Strong signal of **buyers taking control**.

**What to do:** Buy on the close of the green candle with a stop below its low.

### 5. Bearish Engulfing

The opposite — a large red candle engulfs the previous green candle. Strong signal of **sellers taking control**.

**What to do:** Exit longs or short on the close of the red candle.

### 6. Morning Star

A three-candle pattern:
1. Large red candle (sellers in control)
2. Small candle or Doji (indecision)
3. Large green candle closing above the midpoint of candle 1

One of the most **reliable bullish reversal** signals.

### 7. Evening Star

The bearish equivalent of the Morning Star — three candles signaling a reversal from up to down.

### 8. Piercing Line

A green candle that opens below the previous red candle's low but closes above its midpoint. **Moderately bullish** reversal signal.

### 9. Dark Cloud Cover

A red candle that opens above the previous green candle's high but closes below its midpoint. **Moderately bearish** reversal signal.

### 10. Three White Soldiers

Three consecutive large green candles with small wicks, each closing higher than the last. Very **strong bullish momentum** signal.

### 11. Three Black Crows

Three consecutive large red candles, each closing lower than the last. Very **strong bearish momentum** signal.

### 12. Spinning Top

Similar to a Doji — small body with wicks on both sides. Signals **indecision** but less extreme than a Doji.

## How to use these patterns on NSE/BSE

Here's a practical framework:

**Step 1 — Identify the trend first.** A Hammer in a downtrend is meaningful. A Hammer in the middle of an uptrend is not.

**Step 2 — Find the pattern at a key level.** A Bullish Engulfing at a major support level is far more powerful than one in the middle of nowhere.

**Step 3 — Wait for confirmation.** One candle is not enough. Wait for the next candle to move in the expected direction before entering.

**Step 4 — Use volume to confirm.** A reversal candle with 2x average volume is much more reliable than one with below-average volume.

**Step 5 — Set your stop loss.** For bullish patterns, the stop goes below the low of the pattern. For bearish patterns, above the high.

## Common mistakes Indian traders make

- **Trading patterns in isolation** — without checking the broader trend
- **Acting on every Doji** — Dojis are common; only significant ones at key levels matter
- **Ignoring volume** — a pattern without volume confirmation is weak
- **Using only 1-minute charts** — patterns on daily charts are far more reliable than those on intraday charts
